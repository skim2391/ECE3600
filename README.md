New York
1. New York County does not currently have clear county-level data center regulations. However, New York State and New York City are actively exploring regulatory approaches, including proposed laws that require studying and reporting the impacts of data center development before construction.
2. Tompkins County has not yet established formal data center regulations, but the Town of Dryden is taking early steps by defining data centers within local law and exploring potential restrictions.


California
1. Imperial County is a strong example of a county moving toward a formal regulatory framework through its draft Lithium Valley Specific Plan, which includes considerations for large-scale energy and data center development.
2. Santa Clara County does not yet show a clear countywide regulatory framework specifically for data centers. However, within the county, San José is actively shaping data center development through zoning controls, project review processes, and coordination with utilities.


Illinois
1. Champaign County is actively considering a 12-month moratorium on data center permits while a county task force develops new zoning and permitting standards for large data centers.
2. McLean County is moving toward more formal regulation, and recent 2026 discussions show a more developed and detailed approach, including zoning restrictions and case-by-case review of infrastructure impacts.


Ohio
1. In Delaware County, regulatory movement is most visible in Sunbury, where a temporary moratorium on data center development has been proposed through early 2027 while the city evaluates zoning, safety, infrastructure, and community impacts.
2. Pickaway County is another strong example, where Madison Township has already adopted a 12-month moratorium on new data center development while studying impacts and considering zoning changes.


Florida
1. Florida has begun developing statewide policies targeting large-scale data centers. Proposed regulations define large data centers based on high energy use and include requirements related to water use permits, electrical infrastructure costs, and public transparency.
2. At the county level, Orange County restricts data centers primarily to industrial zones and limits expansion into rural or agricultural land. Local policies also emphasize that developers must cover the costs of infrastructure upgrades, including power transmission and substations.


Analysis
1. Across all five states, a common pattern is that data centers are rarely treated as by-right developments. Most counties require additional approval processes such as special use permits or temporary moratoriums to control development. Another shared trend is the strong focus on infrastructure and environmental impact, especially related to electricity demand, water usage, and effects on nearby communities.
2. At the same time, there are clear differences in how developed these regulations are. Some counties, such as McLean County and parts of California, have more structured and established systems. Others, such as Champaign County, Tompkins County, and parts of Ohio, are still in earlier stages and are actively developing new rules or experimenting with temporary restrictions.
3. We also observed that local conditions play a major role. Counties with more available land or energy resources tend to be more open to development, while counties facing infrastructure limits, environmental concerns, or strong community response tend to adopt stricter or more cautious approaches.
4. Overall, even within the same state, counties can regulate data centers in very different ways, which highlights the importance of county-level analysis.
